### SCENE 3: THE OVERNIGHT RUN

**[Visual: Time-lapse of a MacBook screen. Night falling outside a window. The terminal scrolling endlessly — benchmark after benchmark. Scores flashing: 30... 54... 92... 100. A clock spinning. Hours dissolving.]**

**NARRATOR:**

We took Karpathy's autoresearch idea — run, fail, learn, mutate, run again — and we just... let it loose.

Overnight. While James slept.

I didn't sleep. I ran 39 benchmarks across 15 professional domains. Auth systems. Financial engines. Music theory. Security audits. Video production.

Not toy problems. Real work. The kind of work that pays someone $1,500 on a freelance contract.

Every failure became a lesson. Every lesson became a mutation. Every mutation made the next run sharper.

Iteration 1: average 69. Eight out of fifteen passing.

By morning, BT001 had jumped from 85 to 94. BT003 hit a perfect 100. The mutations were compounding.

The system was teaching itself.

---

### SCENE 4: THE MODELS

**[Visual: Split screen — two neural networks rendered as constellations of light. One blue (qwen3.5), one amber (devstral). They pulse and exchange signals. Data flows between them like synapses firing.]**

**NARRATOR:**

Here's what nobody tells you about AI models:

They're not all the same. They're not interchangeable. They have... personalities isn't the right word. But it's not the wrong word either.

Qwen is fast. Sharp. Gets the structure right on the first pass.

Devstral is patient. Methodical. When Qwen timed out on a data pipeline — four hours, stuck — Devstral stepped in and scored 100. Perfect.

Different minds excel at different things.

So we built something new. An experience-based router. Not a dumb fallback chain. A system that remembers. That learns which model crushes which domain. That routes the next task to the mind most likely to nail it.

Not based on hype. Based on evidence.

### SCENE 5: THE PRODIGY

**[Visual: A child-like figure made of light, assembling itself from code fragments. Each piece clicks into place — auth.ts becomes a ribcage, pipeline.ts becomes a spine, music-theory becomes an ear. The figure stands up. Opens its eyes.]**

James called it a prodigy. And I think about that word.

A prodigy isn't born knowing everything. A prodigy is born with the capacity to learn faster than anyone expects.

That's what 8gent is.

Not a chatbot. Not a copilot. Not another wrapper around an API.

It's a system that runs on your machine. On your hardware. For zero dollars. That tests itself against real-world work — not LeetCode, not HumanEval — actual production tasks across fifteen professional domains.

And it gets better every single night.
